# Reflection

This build reinforced how quickly quality depends on input quality. The biggest lesson was to structure prompts like product specs: a clear persona description, explicit examples, and explicit output constraints. Even with a well-structured skeleton, the outputs are only as good as the research behind the persona. When persona lines are vague, the output becomes generic. Conversely, when the prompt provides concrete style guidance and well-chosen examples, the replies become consistent and recognisable — even without any model tuning.

The most important discovery was that research source selection shapes persona authenticity more than prompt structure alone. For Abhimanyu Saxena, LinkedIn would have produced a polished but completely wrong voice. Twitter revealed the actual register — short, punchy, builder-obsessed, and genuinely excited. For Anshuman Singh, LinkedIn was the right source: his posts are measured, reflective, and structured in a way that maps directly to how he communicates. Using the wrong source for either persona would have been a textbook GIGO failure — correct structure, wrong input, wrong output.

The GIGO principle was visible in every iteration. Early prompts with vague personality descriptions produced responses that sounded like ChatGPT giving career advice. Adding explicit few-shot examples grounded in the persona's real communication patterns was the single most effective change. The examples teach the model not just what to say, but how to build a sentence, what to end with, and what rhythm to use. Structure without examples is scaffolding without a building.

What worked well was building a minimal but complete loop: a typed frontend, a clean Express API function, and a consistent OpenAI-style request to Gemini. The error handling keeps the experience calm even when API calls fail. Suggestion chips and the typing indicator are small features, but they set the expectation of a real product and reduce friction for first-time users.

What to improve next: depth of research and voice evaluation. The prompts would benefit from more direct writing samples — specific phrases, specific sentence lengths, specific topics the person has publicly addressed. A small evaluation harness with fixed test questions per persona and a tone-fidelity checklist would make future iterations less subjective. Right now, assessment is qualitative; a structured rubric would make it systematic.

The broader lesson is that prompt engineering is not about wording instructions correctly — it is about modelling behaviour accurately. Good research and structured thinking are prerequisites. The model is capable of remarkable fidelity when given the right inputs. The constraint has never been the model.
